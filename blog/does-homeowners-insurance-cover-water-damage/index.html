Does Homeowners Insurance Cover Water Damage in Texas?

The short answer: usually yes for sudden accidents, usually no for gradual leaks or flooding. Here's the detail.

Water damage is one of the most common — and most misunderstood — homeowners claims. This guide breaks down how coverage generally works in Texas so you know what to expect. (It's general information, not policy advice; always check your own policy and talk to your agent.)

The golden rule: sudden and accidental

Standard homeowners policies are built to cover losses that are sudden and accidental. A pipe that bursts without warning, a water heater that fails, a supply line that suddenly lets go, or a washing machine hose that ruptures — these are typically covered. The damage was unexpected and happened quickly, which is exactly what insurance is designed for.

What's usually NOT covered

  • Gradual leaks and long-term seepage. If a slow drip went unnoticed under a sink for months, insurers often deny it as a maintenance issue rather than a sudden event.
  • Lack of maintenance. Damage traced to neglected plumbing, an obviously aging water heater, or deferred repairs is frequently excluded.
  • Flooding from rising water. This is the big one — damage from external flooding (a swollen creek, flash flooding, water rising up from the ground) is not covered by a standard homeowners policy. That requires separate flood insurance, typically through the NFIP or a private flood policy.
  • Sewer backup — sometimes. Backups are often excluded unless you added a specific sewer/water backup endorsement.

The tricky part: flood vs. storm water in San Angelo

West Texas gives us both flash flooding and severe hail-and-wind storms, and the distinction matters. If a hailstorm punches through your roof and rain pours into your attic, that water damage is usually covered as a storm/wind loss. But if a flash flood sends water rising in from outside, that's a flood — and it needs a flood policy. The source and path of the water determines coverage, which is exactly why documentation is so important.

How to give your claim the best chance

  1. Document immediately. Photos and video of the damage and the source, before cleanup begins.
  2. Report promptly. Most policies require timely notice — call your insurer as soon as you reasonably can.
  3. Mitigate the damage. Policies actually require you to take reasonable steps to prevent further damage (like stopping the water and starting drying). This is another reason to call a restoration pro fast — professional mitigation is expected.
  4. Keep records. Save receipts, damaged-item lists, and the restoration company's moisture logs and scope of work.
  5. Let the pros document, too. The restoration pros we connect you with produce photos, moisture readings and an itemized scope your adjuster can rely on.

What about the deductible?

Even a covered claim is subject to your deductible. For smaller losses, it's worth weighing the repair cost against your deductible and the potential effect on your premium. A free estimate helps you make that call before you file — which is one more reason to get a professional assessment early.

Bottom line for Concho Valley homeowners

Sudden, accidental water damage from inside your home is usually covered; gradual leaks and outside flooding usually aren't. The best thing you can do is act fast, document thoroughly, and mitigate the damage — all of which a professional restoration response supports.
