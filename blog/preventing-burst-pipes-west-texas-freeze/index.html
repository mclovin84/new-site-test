Preventing Burst Pipes During a West Texas Freeze

San Angelo doesn't freeze often — which is exactly why a hard freeze catches so many homes off guard.

We all remember what a serious cold snap can do to Texas plumbing. Because deep freezes are relatively rare here, many San Angelo homes aren't built or insulated for them — pipes run through exterior walls, attics and unconditioned garages that a northern home would never allow. When temperatures plunge and stay below freezing, those pipes are vulnerable. The good news: a little preparation goes a long way.

Why pipes burst

It's a common misconception that pipes burst simply because ice expands against them. What actually happens is more mechanical: as water freezes and expands, it creates enormous pressure between the ice blockage and a closed faucet. That trapped pressure is what ruptures the pipe — often at a weak point — and the real damage comes when everything thaws and water pours out of the break. That's why a frozen pipe can look fine until the thaw.

Before a freeze: prepare your home

  • Insulate exposed pipes in the garage, attic, crawl space and along exterior walls with foam pipe sleeves — cheap and available at any hardware store.
  • Disconnect garden hoses and cover outdoor spigots with insulated faucet covers. A connected hose traps water right at the bib.
  • Know where your main shutoff is and make sure it works. In an emergency, shutting off the water fast is your best defense.
  • Seal drafts near pipes — gaps around where lines enter the house let freezing air right in.
  • Service your heat before winter so you're not without it during the coldest nights.

During a hard freeze: the overnight routine

  • Let faucets drip. A slow trickle from faucets on exterior walls relieves pressure and keeps water moving, which resists freezing. This is the single most effective overnight habit.
  • Open cabinet doors under sinks so household heat reaches the pipes, especially on exterior walls.
  • Keep the heat on and set no lower than around 55°F, even if you're away.
  • Open the garage door only as needed — if water lines run through the garage, keep it closed to hold in warmth.

If a pipe freezes

If you turn on a faucet and only a trickle comes out, you likely have a frozen section. Keep that faucet open (so melting water can escape and relieve pressure) and gently warm the pipe with a hair dryer or towels soaked in warm water, working from the faucet end toward the blockage. Never use an open flame. If you can't locate the frozen spot, can't reach it, or suspect the pipe has already burst, shut off the main water supply immediately.

If a pipe bursts: act fast

A burst pipe can release a lot of water quickly. Shut off the main water supply, then the electricity to the affected area if water is near outlets or your panel. Document the damage with photos, and call for professional help right away — the faster extraction and drying begin, the less of your home is affected. This is a classic sudden-and-accidental loss that's typically covered by homeowners insurance (see our insurance guide).
